# Written Assignments

## Part 1: SESMag and Jailbreaking / Slider Activity

SESMag helped us think about software users through social and economic context instead of assuming that everyone has the same technology, time, confidence, or risk level. The DAV persona was especially important because DAV represents a user who may be digitally harmed by unreliable access, older hardware, weak networks, confusing interfaces, or systems where one mistake can have a large cost. In the class activity, the Reliable Technology Slider made this concrete: moving the slider left meant designing for limited bandwidth, low-end devices, text-first interactions, and clear feedback, while moving it right meant assuming more reliable devices, stronger networks, and more tolerance for complex interfaces. The assets in the SESMag study were useful because they gave us a structured way to discuss what each persona needs, what technology they can rely on, and how design choices can either reduce or increase barriers.

The jailbreaking and LLM role-play activity was helpful but not completely easy. It was useful because asking an AI to role-play DAV forced us to ask more empathetic questions about access, stress, and mistakes instead of only asking technical questions. It was hard because LLMs can generalize too much, avoid direct role-play, or invent details that may not match the study, so the prompts needed careful framing and follow-up questions. If we changed the activity, we would give students a short checklist for validating the LLM's answers against SESMag sources, require each team to compare ChatGPT and Gemini outputs side by side, and add a reflection step where students identify one concrete design decision they would change after hearing from the DAV perspective.

## Part 2: SESMag Architecture Analysis

Our Angular, Node.js, Express, PostgreSQL, and AI-assisted architecture fits SESMag because it separates user needs instead of forcing every persona into the same interface. The applicant portal is intentionally minimalist, responsive, and text-based, which supports DAV by reducing cognitive load, lowering bandwidth requirements, and making important actions like Save and Undo highly visible. Angular gives us reusable components for forms, scheduling, and chat without requiring heavy graphics, while Tailwind helps keep the interface responsive and consistent. PostgreSQL is a good fit because HR data is relational: applicants, managers, departments, jobs, skills, schedules, and applications all need clear structure and safe updates.

The asymmetric design also supports managers without sacrificing DAV needs. Managers receive a data-rich dashboard with aggregate applicant counts, department load, open shift slots, and candidate matches, while applicants get a calmer workflow focused on profile editing and availability. The AI matchmaking assistant is especially useful for DAV because it allows a student to type natural language such as "I am free on Tuesday mornings" instead of learning a complex search form. The backend keeps the design safe by letting AI extract criteria only, while Express and PostgreSQL control the actual query logic. This gives the project the benefits of AI without giving the model direct control over sensitive HR data.
